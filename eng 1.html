Abstract

The LESBIAN CRYPTID (LC) uses infrared cameras, humidity/temperature and oxygen/gas sensors, an accelerometer, and soil samples to characterize the inside of a cave that is inaccessible for a human. With its spherical design and distribution of mass, the LC is able to easily roll into cave crevices. Unfortunately, we were unable to get the oxygen/gas sensor functional for our deployment, but we were able to collect images, accelerometer, humidity and temperature data, and soil samples over the course of two deployments.

Design

As design lead, Maxine was responsible for organizing presentation and visual materials as well as the robotic arm component. When the LC finishes rolling into the cave, the robotic arm reaches out of the ball and touches an adhesive strip to the ground, collecting a soil sample from the cave surface.

The arm mechanism unwinds from a servo controlled by one of the device’s Raspberry Pis. Two spools are attached to an axle connected at the top of cardboard side walls. The pulley servo-head connects to another pulley, on the same axle, with a rubber band. As the servo spins, the axle rotates. After rotating in the first direction for three seconds the pulley delays for a moment and then rewinds the arm, see figure 1. The movement of the arm is guided by gravity and a ramp-like casing assembled with laser cut cardboard, see figure 2. The entire mechanism fits flush against an opening in the LC’s acrylic casing, see figure 3.
